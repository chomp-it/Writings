Do not you?

Sometimes in programming, we run into the ‘Don’t you?’ vs ‘Do you not?’ case; and it’s quite hard to defend one way to do it when you look at it this way.

Nobody says “Do not you?” —but if we had some magical counter for how many times we’ve said “Don’t you . . .?”, It would be quite high. 

The thing is, that hidden ugliness of Don’t you can be ignored entirely! We don’t say unless . . . else — we just say if else.

However, we don’t say “you write OCaml, do you not?” when we talk to a new person. We say “Hey! You write OCaml, don’t you?”. Notice the difference? So why shouldn’t we say “unless true” instead of “if not false”?

“Do you not want to go on a walk with me?” and “Don’t you want to go on a walk with me” carry two extremely different meanings. One is scared, nervous, and concerned: do you want to? It’s okay if you don’t — and the other is saying: “Cmon! Let’s go for a walk!”

And guess what? That’s how you use unless; if you are assuming negation/minority/being pessimistic.

Is the glass half full or is it half empty? I don’t know! But what I do know is that unless and don’t you think it’s half empty, and do you not and if see it as half full.
